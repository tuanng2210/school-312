About The Team

The Blue Team (Italian: "Squadra azzurra") represented Italy in international contract bridge tournaments, winning sixteen world titles from 1957 through 1975. From 1964 to 1969 and during a 1972 comeback, the team comprised three regular pairs: Walter Avarelli–Giorgio Belladonna, Pietro Forquet–Benito Garozzo, and Massimo D'Alelio–Camillo Pabis Ticci. Eugenio Chiaradia and Guglielmo Siniscalco played in early years; Dano De Falco, Arturo Franco, and Vito Pittalà in late years. The spiritual father, long-time coach, and non-playing captain through 1966 was Carl'Alberto Perroux. The Italian Blue squad was, without a doubt, the best bridge squad ever. After dominating the bridge world for 15 years, they took a vacation for a few years and came back to win three more Bermuda Bowls and an Olympiad. The fact that these competitions are taking place in Italy made it an ideal time to recognize this outstanding squad.

History

The "Blue Team" defeated the "Red Team" in 1956 Italian team trials, which gave the club its name. Before that, Perroux had begun plans to win European championships and the Bermuda Bowl, dominated by American teams. Perroux's side won European titles in 1951, 1956, and 1957, but lost the 1951 Bermuda Bowl in Naples to the US. Perroux remained confident and prepared for the most effective bridge team ever. The triumph came in 1957 when Italy defeated the U.S. in the Bermuda Bowl final in New York. Massimo d'Alelio, Walter Avarelli, Giorgio Belladonna, Eugenio Chiaradia, Pietro Forquet, and Guglielmo Siniscalco played. That was just the beginning—the Blue Team would rule for 12 of the next 13 years, interrupted only by a disappointing 1960 Olympiad. The Blue Team's success relied on novel bidding methods, which conservative Americans found odd. Losing teams grumbled that Italian bids were partly incomprehensible to Americans, giving them an unfair advantage. Belladonna of Rome and his partners played Roman club, a "short club" style, while Neapolitans Forquet and Chiaradia played strong club. Both systems opened with canapés and light bids and comments. In 1960, young, inventive, and cocky Garozzo joined the team and developed the Neapolitan system into the Blue Club, which became famous worldwide. Blue Team won until 1969, when they retired. The Dallas Aces won the 1970 and 1971 Bermuda Bowls without the Blue Team. The reformed Blue Team defeated the Americans in the 1972 Olympiad, 1973, and 1974 Bermuda Bowls. In 1975, Italy defeated the USA again after the Aces dissolved in 1974. USA won the Bermuda Bowl and Brazil won the Olympiad in 1976, ending the Blue Team's supremacy. After a string of wins, Forquet, Pabis-Ticci, and D'Alelio withdrew, and Garozzo and Belladonna played C. Garozzo's "Super Precision" Precision Club, C. Wei's. The "Blue Team" won despite losing its name. Facchini-Zucchelli's "foot-tapping scandal" tainted 1975's win.
